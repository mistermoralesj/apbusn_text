Lesson 2.1: Marketing to Customers

Customer Data, Segmentation, Target Customers, Customer Profiles, and Customer Relationships

Why This Lesson Matters

A business cannot market to everyone. When a business tries to appeal to everybody, its message often becomes too vague to matter. Strong marketing begins with understanding who the customer is, what they value, and how the business can build a relationship with that customer.

Essential Question

How do businesses use customer data and segmentation to identify the right customers?

What Is Marketing?

Marketing is the process of understanding customers, creating value, communicating value, and delivering products to customers. Marketing is not just advertising — it also includes researching customer needs, identifying target customers, studying competitors, designing products, setting prices, choosing sales channels, promoting products, and building customer relationships.

Why Marketers Collect Customer Data

Customer data is information about customers, their characteristics, needs, behaviors, and preferences. Businesses collect it because guessing is risky. Good data helps answer: Who is most likely to buy? What problem needs solving? How much will the customer pay? Where do they shop? What message gets their attention?

Real-World Example: Spotify

Spotify collects data about what users play, skip, save, replay, and add to playlists. That data helps Spotify recommend songs, create playlists, keep users engaged, and encourage free users to become paid subscribers. The customer data supports both product and marketing decisions.

Types of Customer Data

Type of Data Meaning Example
Demographic Data Basic information about who customers are. Age, grade level, income, family size, location
Psychographic Data Values, interests, lifestyles, and motivations. Health-focused, budget-conscious, tech-loving
Behavioral Data What customers do. Purchases, website clicks, app usage, search history

Privacy, Identity Theft, and Data Risk

Customer data is valuable, but it also creates risk. Risks include privacy infringement, identity theft, financial fraud, data breaches, loss of customer trust, and legal consequences. Businesses should collect only the data they need, protect customer information, explain how data is used, and avoid misleading customers.

Market Segmentation

Market segmentation is the process of dividing a large market into smaller groups of customers with shared characteristics. Customers are not all the same — one group may care most about performance, another about price, another about style.

Common Segmentation Categories

Segmentation Type What It Looks At Example
Demographic Who customers are Teenagers, parents, college students, retirees
Geographic Where customers live or shop Central Florida, urban areas, school campuses
Psychographic What customers value or believe Eco-conscious, convenience-focused, budget-minded
Behavioral How customers act Buys weekly, uses coupons, prefers subscriptions

Target Customers

A target customer is the specific customer group a business focuses on serving. A clear target customer helps the business make decisions about product features, price, branding, promotions, sales channels, and customer relationships.

Weak: Everyone who likes food.

Stronger: Student athletes who stay after school and need affordable snacks before practice.

Customer Profile

A customer profile is a detailed description of the target customer, including age or grade level, location, daily routines, interests, values, problems, buying habits, price sensitivity, current alternatives, and reasons they might buy or hesitate.

Customer Profile Example:

Our target customer is a high school student athlete who stays after school several days per week. This customer wants a quick snack before practice but has limited time and money. They value convenience, affordability, and food that gives them energy without feeling too heavy.

Customer Relationships

Customer relationships describe how a business attracts, serves, communicates with, and keeps customers — through friendly service, loyalty rewards, personalized recommendations, email or text updates, social media engagement, and membership programs. Strong relationships lead to repeat purchases, referrals, and loyalty.

Product-Market Fit

Concept Main Question
Problem-Solution Fit Does the product solve a real problem?
Product-Market Fit Do enough customers want it enough to make the business work?

Business Canvas Connection

Target Customer Profile Frame:

Our target customer is __________. This customer struggles with __________ and values __________. We believe this customer is a strong fit because __________.

Customer Relationship Frame:

We will build customer relationships by __________ because our target customers need __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Marketing is more than advertising.
  • Customer data includes demographic, psychographic, and behavioral information.
  • Customer data creates privacy and security risks.
  • Market segmentation divides a large market into smaller customer groups.
  • A target customer is the specific group a business focuses on serving.
  • A customer profile describes the target customer in detail.
  • Product-market fit means customer demand is strong enough to support the business.

Lesson 2.2: Consumer Behavior

Buying Decisions, Consumer Psychology, Social Influence, and Sales Tactics

Why This Lesson Matters

Customers do not always buy products for purely logical reasons. Buying decisions can be shaped by habits, emotions, price, convenience, friends, family, culture, social media, brand image, and personal values. If businesses understand consumer behavior, they can make better decisions about products, pricing, promotion, and customer relationships.

Essential Question

How do psychological, social, and personal factors influence what customers buy?

The Buying Decision Process

Stage What Happens Example
Problem Recognition The customer realizes they have a problem, need, or want. A student is hungry before practice.
Information Search The customer looks for possible solutions. The student checks vending machines, cafeteria options, or nearby stores.
Evaluation of Alternatives The customer compares choices. The student compares price, distance, speed, and food quality.
Purchase Decision The customer decides what to buy. The student buys a snack box.
Post-Purchase Evaluation The customer decides if the purchase was worth it. The student decides if they would buy again.

Psychological Factors

Psychological factors are internal thoughts, feelings, motivations, and perceptions — including motivation, perception, attitudes, beliefs, past experiences, emotions, and brand trust. A customer may choose one restaurant over another because of a good past experience, or avoid a brand because of negative reviews.

Social Factors

Social factors are influences from other people and groups — friends, family, classmates, teams, influencers, and online communities. Social factors are powerful because people often look to others when deciding what is popular, trusted, or worth buying.

Real-World Example: Stanley Cups

Stanley cups became popular partly because of social influence. People saw the product on social media, in school, at work, and among friends. The product did not only compete as a water bottle — it became part of a lifestyle and trend.

Personal and Cultural Factors

Personal factors are individual characteristics that shape buying decisions: age, income, schedule, lifestyle, personality, values, health needs, and budget. Cultural factors include shared traditions, norms, beliefs, and habits. Businesses must be careful not to stereotype — strong marketers use research and evidence rather than assumptions.

Sales Tactics

Sales tactics are strategies businesses use to persuade customers to buy. They can be helpful when they clearly communicate value, and become unethical when they mislead, pressure, or manipulate customers.

Sales Tactic Example Why It Works
Scarcity Only 20 snack boxes available today. Customers may act quickly if supply feels limited.
Social Proof Over 100 students ordered this week. Customers may trust a product more if others use it.
Bundling Snack, drink, and fruit cup sold together. Customers may feel they are getting more value.
Loyalty Rewards Buy five boxes, get one free. Customers have a reason to return.

Ethical Sales Tactics

A sales tactic is more ethical when it provides accurate information, does not pressure vulnerable customers, does not hide costs, does not exaggerate benefits, and respects customer choice. Saying "limited supply" is ethical if supply is actually limited — it becomes misleading if used only to pressure customers.

Business Canvas Connection

Sales Tactic Response Frame:

Our business will use __________ as a sales tactic because our target customer is influenced by __________. This tactic supports our value proposition by __________.

Example: Our business will use loyalty rewards because our target customer is influenced by convenience and repeat routines. This encourages student athletes to rely on us for after-school snacks each week.

Key Takeaways

  • Consumer behavior studies how people decide what to buy, use, and recommend.
  • Buying decisions can be influenced by psychological, social, personal, and cultural factors.
  • Customers often move through a five-stage buying decision process.
  • Social influence can strongly affect customer choices.
  • Sales tactics should be accurate, fair, and ethical.

Lesson 2.3: Market Research

Primary Research, Secondary Research, Customer Preferences, Competitive Research, and Data Visualization

Why This Lesson Matters

Good marketing decisions should not rely on guesses. Market research helps businesses understand customers, competitors, and trends before making major decisions. Strong market research can help a business avoid wasting money on products, prices, features, or campaigns that customers do not want.

Essential Question

How can businesses use research to understand customers and make better marketing decisions?

Primary vs. Secondary Research

Type Meaning Strength Weakness
Primary Research Data you collect yourself — surveys, interviews, observations, experiments. Specific to your business question. Can take time to collect.
Secondary Research Data already collected by others — industry reports, news, competitor sites. Often faster and broader. May not perfectly match your question.

Real-World Example: New Coke

A product may perform well in a taste test but still fail if customers have emotional connections to the original brand, packaging, or tradition. Market research should study the full customer experience, not only one feature.

Writing Strong Research Questions

Weak Question Stronger Question Why It Is Stronger
Do you like snacks? How many times per week do you buy a snack after school? It gives measurable behavior.
Would you buy our amazing product? What price range would you consider reasonable for this product? Avoids leading language; helps pricing decisions.

Research Samples

A sample is the group of people who respond to a survey or research activity. The sample should match the target customer. If your target customer is student athletes, collect data from student athletes — not just random classmates.

Competitive Research

Competitive research means studying direct competitors (similar products), indirect competitors (solving the same problem differently), and substitutes customers already use. Example: If your business sells after-school snack boxes, direct competitors include vending machines and nearby stores; indirect competitors include students bringing snacks from home.

Data Visualization

Data visualization uses charts, graphs, or visuals to present data clearly. A good visualization should be easy to read and connected to a business decision.

Weak: A chart with no title, unclear labels, and too many categories.

Stronger: A bar chart titled "Preferred Snack Box Price Range" with clear price options and response counts.

Business Canvas Connection

Research Findings Frame:

We researched __________ to learn __________. Our findings showed __________. Based on this evidence, our business will __________.

Example: We researched snack preferences to learn what student athletes would want. Our findings showed students preferred protein snacks, fruit, and drinks under $5. Based on this evidence, our business will offer a low-cost snack box with a protein option, fruit, and a drink.

Key Takeaways

  • Market research helps businesses make evidence-based decisions.
  • Primary research is collected directly from customers.
  • Secondary research uses information already collected by others.
  • Strong research questions are clear, specific, neutral, measurable, and connected to a decision.
  • A sample should match the target customer group.
  • Competitive research studies direct competitors, indirect competitors, and substitutes.
  • Data visualizations help communicate patterns in research findings.

  6. What is a sample?

Lesson 2.4: Product

MVPs, Product-Market Fit, Value Proposition, Branding, and Brand Identity

Why This Lesson Matters

A product is successful when customers see value in it and are willing to use it, buy it, or recommend it. In this lesson, you will learn how businesses develop products, test MVPs, evaluate product-market fit, create value propositions, and build brand identity.

Essential Question

How do businesses develop products customers value and brands customers remember?

Product Features and Benefits

A feature is what a product has or does. A benefit is why that feature matters to the customer. Customers usually care more about benefits than features.

Product Feature Benefit
Water bottle Insulated stainless steel Keeps drinks cold longer.
Planner app Deadline reminders Helps students avoid missing assignments.
Snack box Pre-ordered pickup Saves time before practice.

Minimum Viable Product

A minimum viable product (MVP) is the simplest version of a product that can be shown to customers for feedback — a sketch, sample product, mock website, prototype, menu draft, or demo. The point is not perfection. The point is feedback.

Real-World Example: MVP Testing

If your business idea is a custom school planner app, you don't need to build the full app first. Sketch several screens or create a clickable mockup. If students say the design is confusing, revise before investing time in a full build.

Product-Market Fit

Signs of product-market fit may include: customers say they would use or buy the product, customers recommend it to others, customers return for repeat purchases, customers prefer it over alternatives, and customers are willing to pay a realistic price.

Value Proposition

Value Proposition Frame:

For __________ who struggle with __________, our product provides __________ by __________.

Example: For student athletes who struggle to find affordable food before practice, our snack box provides quick, affordable energy by offering pre-ordered pickup near the gym.

Brand and Brand Identity

A brand is the identity and reputation of a business or product — shaped by name, logo, colors, tone, packaging, customer service, and social media presence. Brand identity is how the business intentionally presents itself. It should match the target customer and value proposition.

Business Canvas Connection

Product Response Frame:

Our MVP was __________. We tested it by __________. Customers responded by __________. Based on this evidence, we will revise our product by __________.

Brand Identity Response Frame:

Our brand identity uses __________ to communicate __________ to our target customer.

Key Takeaways

  • Features describe what a product has or does; benefits explain why features matter.
  • An MVP is the simplest version of a product used to collect feedback.
  • Product-market fit means customer demand is strong enough to support the business.
  • A value proposition explains why customers should choose the product.
  • A brand is the identity and reputation of a business or product.
  • Brand identity should be tested with the target customer.

  3. What is an MVP?
  4. Why do businesses test MVPs?
  5. What is product-market fit?

Lesson 2.5: Price

Pricing Strategy, Costs, Customer Value, Competition, and Price Testing

Why This Lesson Matters

Price is one of the most important decisions a business makes. Too high, and customers may not buy. Too low, and the business may not earn enough to survive. Strong businesses use costs, customer value, competitor prices, and market research to make pricing decisions.

Essential Question

How do businesses set prices that customers accept and the business can sustain?

Fixed Costs and Variable Costs

Fixed costs stay the same regardless of how many products are sold — rent, website hosting, insurance, equipment payments. Variable costs change based on how many products are made or sold — ingredients, packaging, shipping, sales commissions.

Real-World Example: Disney Parks

Disney does not compete mainly by being the cheapest theme park option. It charges premium prices because many customers value the experience, brand, characters, service, and emotional connection. This shows how price connects to perceived value.

Common Pricing Strategies

Pricing Strategy Meaning Example
Cost-Based Set price based on costs plus desired profit. A snack box costs $3 to make and sells for $5.
Value-Based Set price based on what customers believe the product is worth. A premium planner app charges more because it saves time and stress.
Competition-Based Set price based on competitor prices. A smoothie business prices near similar local shops.
Penetration Start low to attract customers quickly. A new subscription app offers a low first-month price.
Premium Charge higher to signal quality or exclusivity. A luxury skincare brand charges more than basic alternatives.

Business Canvas Connection

Pricing Strategy Response Frame:

Our product will be priced at __________. We chose this price because __________. Our pricing strategy is __________ because __________.

Example: Our snack box will be priced at $5. We chose this price because student interviews showed most athletes would pay between $4 and $6. Our pricing strategy is value-based because students care about convenience, time savings, and a complete snack option.

Key Takeaways

  • Businesses must consider costs, customer value, and competitor prices.
  • Fixed costs stay the same regardless of sales volume.
  • Variable costs change based on production or sales volume.
  • Willingness to pay is the amount customers are willing to spend.
  • Pricing affects brand image and customer expectations.
  • Businesses should test price assumptions with target customers.

Check Your Understanding

  1. What is price?
  2. Why is pricing important?
  3. What is the difference between fixed costs and variable costs?
  4. What does willingness to pay mean?
  5. Why should a business study competitor prices?
  6. What is cost-based pricing?
  7. What is value-based pricing?
  8. What is premium pricing?
  9. How can price affect brand image?
  10. Choose a business idea and explain one price you would test with customers.

Lesson 2.6: Place and Channels

Distribution, Marketing Channels, Direct Channels, Indirect Channels, and Omnichannel Strategy

Why This Lesson Matters

A product only creates value if customers can actually access it. A business may have the right product, price, and customer, but still fail if customers cannot easily find, buy, or receive the product.

Essential Question

How do businesses choose the best channels to deliver products to customers?

Direct vs. Indirect Channels

Channel Type Strength Weakness
Direct Channel More control over customer experience and communication. Business must handle more work itself.
Indirect Channel Can reach more customers through partners. Less control and possible fees or commissions.

Real-World Example: Target

Target uses stores, a website, a mobile app, curbside pickup, delivery, and loyalty programs. A customer can browse online, order through the app, pick up in the parking lot, or shop in the store. That is an omnichannel approach because the channels work together.

Channel Conflict

Channel conflict happens when different channels compete or interfere with each other. If a product is sold for $20 on a business website but $30 in a partner store, customers may become frustrated. Businesses should make sure pricing, communication, and service feel consistent across channels.

Business Canvas Connection

Channel Strategy Response Frame:

Our business will reach customers through __________ because our target customers __________. This channel supports our value proposition by __________.

Example: Our business will reach customers through a school pickup table and online pre-order form because our target customers are already on campus after school. This makes snack pickup fast and convenient before practice.

Key Takeaways

  • Place refers to where and how customers access a product or service.
  • A channel is the path a product takes from the business to the customer.
  • Direct channels allow businesses to sell directly to customers.
  • Indirect channels use another business or intermediary.
  • Omnichannel strategy uses multiple connected channels.
  • Channel conflict can occur when channels create inconsistency.

Lesson 2.7: Promotion and Marketing Communications

Promotion, Advertising, Public Relations, Social Media, Content Marketing, and Digital Marketing Tools

Why This Lesson Matters

A business can have the right product, the right price, and the right channel, but customers still need to hear about it. Promotion is how a business communicates value. Strong promotion doesn't simply say "Buy this" — it explains why the product matters, who it helps, and what makes it worth choosing.

Essential Question

How do businesses communicate value to customers and encourage action?

Marketing Communication Methods

Method What It Is Example
Advertising Paid communication to promote a product or brand. Social media ads, YouTube ads, billboards, sponsored posts
Public Relations Efforts to shape how the public sees the business. Press releases, community events, charity partnerships
Sales Promotion Short-term incentives to encourage customer action. Coupons, buy-one-get-one, loyalty cards, free samples
Social Media Marketing Using platforms to communicate with customers. Instagram posts, TikTok videos, YouTube, Snapchat
Content Marketing Helpful or entertaining content to attract customers. How-to videos, blog posts, infographics, customer stories
Digital Marketing Tools Tools to create, send, manage, or measure online marketing. QR codes, email campaigns, analytics dashboards, landing pages

Real-World Example: Community Reputation

A local restaurant may build public goodwill by donating meals during community events or supporting school fundraisers. That activity may not be a direct advertisement, but it can shape how customers feel about the business.

The AIDA Model

Stage Meaning Example
Attention Get the customer to notice the message. A bold poster near the gym.
Interest Make the customer want to learn more. A message explaining quick pickup before practice.
Desire Make the customer want the product. Showing the snack box contents and price.
Action Tell the customer what to do next. Scan the QR code to preorder.

Integrated Marketing Communication

Integrated marketing communication means all promotional messages work together and feel consistent — same product name, logo, colors, tone, value proposition, and call to action across every channel. If messages feel disconnected, customers may become confused or lose trust.

Ethics in Promotion

Ethical promotion avoids false claims, hidden fees, fake reviews, misleading images, exaggerated results, manipulative pressure, and targeting vulnerable customers unfairly. If a snack business advertises a product as "healthy," it should be able to support that claim with ingredients or nutrition information.

Business Canvas Connection

Marketing Campaign Response Frame:

Our marketing campaign will target __________ by communicating __________. We will use __________ as our digital marketing tool because __________.

Example: Our marketing campaign will target student athletes by communicating quick, affordable energy before practice. We will use an Instagram post and QR code preorder form because our target customers use social media and need a fast way to order.

Key Takeaways

  • Promotion communicates value to customers.
  • Advertising is paid; public relations shapes reputation; sales promotions use short-term incentives.
  • Social media marketing helps businesses reach and interact with customers.
  • Content marketing attracts customers through helpful or entertaining content.
  • AIDA stands for Attention, Interest, Desire, and Action.
  • Promotional messages should be clear, consistent, and ethical.
