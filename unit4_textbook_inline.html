Lesson 4.1: Management and Team Roles

Management, Leadership, Team Skills, Roles, Responsibilities, and Accountability

Why This Lesson Matters

A strong business idea can fail if the team cannot execute it. Businesses need people who can plan, organize, communicate, solve problems, and follow through. Management is about turning goals into action. Leadership is about guiding people toward a shared purpose. In small businesses, the same person may do both.

Essential Question

How do managers and teams organize work to help a business succeed?

The Four Management Functions

Function Meaning Business Example
Planning Setting goals and deciding how to reach them. Creating a sales goal for the first month.
Organizing Arranging people, tasks, and resources. Assigning one person to marketing and another to finance.
Leading Motivating and guiding people. Helping the team stay focused before a pitch.
Controlling Measuring progress and making corrections. Checking sales data and adjusting the marketing plan.

Management vs. Leadership

Management focuses on systems, planning, organization, and results. Leadership focuses on direction, motivation, communication, and influence. A team with leadership but no management may feel inspired but disorganized. A team with management but no leadership may be organized but unmotivated. Businesses need both.

Team Roles

A team role is a responsibility assigned to a person based on the needs of the business and the strengths of the team member. Roles should not be random — they should match skills, interests, and project needs.

Role Primary Responsibility
Project Manager Keeps the team organized and tracks deadlines.
Marketing Lead Handles customer research, branding, and promotion.
Finance Lead Tracks costs, pricing, revenue, and financial statements.
Operations Lead Plans production, supply chain, and delivery.
Presentation Lead Organizes the pitch and speaking roles.
Research Lead Collects evidence from customers and competitors.

Business Example

A student team creating a snack box business assigns roles by strength: the number-savvy member handles pricing and the income statement, the design-oriented member handles branding, and the most organized member manages deadlines and presentation planning. The business runs better because roles match team strengths.

Skills Assessment and Accountability

A skills assessment helps a team identify what each member does well — communication, organization, public speaking, writing, research, math and finance, design, technology, customer service, and problem-solving. Accountability means team members are responsible for completing their work and communicating honestly about progress. Strong teams assign clear roles, set deadlines, check progress often, communicate problems early, and document decisions.

Business Canvas Connection

Team Skills Response Frame:

Our team's strongest skills are __________, __________, and __________. These skills support our business because __________. Our team still needs support with __________, so we will __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Management helps businesses plan, organize, lead, and control resources.
  • Leadership helps guide and motivate people toward a shared goal.
  • Businesses need both management and leadership.
  • Team roles should match business needs and team member strengths.
  • Skills assessments help teams divide work effectively.
  • Accountability helps teams meet deadlines and complete work responsibly.

Lesson 4.2: Measuring Business Performance

Goals, Metrics, KPIs, Operational Performance, Financial Performance, and Dashboards

Why This Lesson Matters

Businesses need to know whether they are making progress. A business owner cannot simply say, "I think things are going well." They need evidence. Performance measures help businesses track progress, identify problems, and make better decisions.

Essential Question

How do businesses measure performance and use data to improve?

Goals, Metrics, and KPIs

A goal is something a business wants to achieve. A metric is a measurement used to track progress. A key performance indicator (KPI) is an important metric tied directly to a business goal. All KPIs are metrics, but not all metrics are KPIs — a KPI should be important enough to guide decisions.

Business Goal Possible Metric / KPI
Increase sales Number of units sold
Improve customer satisfaction Average customer rating
Reduce delivery problems Percentage of orders delivered on time
Improve marketing reach Number of views, clicks, or sign-ups

Operational vs. Financial KPIs

Operational KPIs measure how well the business runs day to day — production time, order accuracy, delivery time, inventory turnover, customer wait time, defect rate, and employee productivity. Financial KPIs measure money-related performance — revenue, profit, net income, gross margin, cost per unit, average order value, break-even point, and cash flow.

Business Example

A student-run snack box business tracks two KPIs: Operational KPI: percentage of boxes prepared correctly. Financial KPI: profit per box. If boxes are accurate but profit is low, the business may need to adjust pricing or reduce costs. If profit is strong but orders are often wrong, the business needs to improve operations.

Dashboards

A dashboard is a visual display of important performance measures that helps managers quickly see what is going well and what needs attention. A good dashboard focuses on the most important measures — too many numbers can create confusion. KPIs are useful only if the business uses them to make decisions.

Business Canvas Connection

KPI Response Frame:

Our operational KPI is __________ because it helps us measure __________. Our financial KPI is __________ because it helps us measure __________. We will use these KPIs to decide __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Business performance measures how well a business achieves its goals.
  • A metric is a measurement used to track progress.
  • A KPI is an important metric tied to a business goal.
  • Operational KPIs measure day-to-day business performance.
  • Financial KPIs measure money-related performance.
  • Dashboards visually summarize important performance measures.
  • KPIs should help businesses make better decisions.

Lesson 4.3: Decision-Making and Problem Solving

Business Decisions, Trade-Offs, Risk, Evidence, and Strategic Thinking

Why This Lesson Matters

Every business decision involves trade-offs. When a business chooses one option, it usually gives up another. Managers constantly make decisions about hiring, pricing, marketing, expansion, technology, financing, and operations. Good decision-making does not guarantee success, but it increases the likelihood of positive outcomes.

Essential Question

How do business leaders make informed decisions when facing uncertainty?

The Business Decision-Making Process

Strong business decisions rarely happen by guessing. Most successful organizations follow a process: (1) Identify the problem. (2) Gather information. (3) Develop possible solutions. (4) Evaluate advantages and disadvantages. (5) Select the best option. (6) Implement the decision. (7) Evaluate results. This process helps businesses reduce risk and avoid emotional decision-making.

Risk and Trade-Offs

Risk is the possibility that a decision may not produce the expected outcome. Business decisions always involve some level of uncertainty — customer preferences changing, new competitors, economic downturns, supply chain disruptions, and technology changes. A trade-off occurs when choosing one option requires giving up another.

Decision Potential Benefit Trade-Off
Lower prices More customers Lower profit per sale
Hire more workers Higher production capacity Higher payroll expenses
Increase advertising Greater awareness Higher marketing costs

Case Example

A local bakery can either purchase a new oven or spend the same money on advertising. The oven may increase production capacity. The advertising may increase customer demand. Since the bakery cannot afford both, management must evaluate which decision better supports business goals.

Decision Trees

A decision tree is a visual tool that helps managers evaluate options and possible outcomes, comparing potential rewards, risks, different decision paths, and alternative strategies. Many organizations use decision trees when evaluating major investments or expansion opportunities.

Business Canvas Connection

Decision-Making Frame:

Our business considered __________ and __________. We selected __________ because evidence showed __________. The primary trade-off is __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Every business decision involves trade-offs.
  • A structured decision-making process helps reduce risk and emotional bias.
  • Evidence should support business decisions whenever possible.
  • Risk is the possibility that a decision may not produce the expected outcome.
  • Decision trees help managers visualize options and possible outcomes.
  • Good managers identify risks and create contingency plans.

Check Your Understanding

  1. Why do business decisions always involve trade-offs?
  2. What are the seven steps of the business decision-making process?
  3. What is risk in a business context?
  4. Give one example of a trade-off a business might face.

  6. Why should business decisions be based on evidence rather than guesses?

Lesson 4.4: SWOT Analysis

Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats, and Strategic Planning

Why This Lesson Matters

Businesses must understand what they do well, where they struggle, what opportunities exist, and what threats could affect future success. One of the most widely used strategic planning tools is SWOT analysis.

Essential Question

How can SWOT analysis help businesses make strategic decisions?

What Is SWOT Analysis?

SWOT Analysis is a strategic planning framework that evaluates Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats. It helps businesses understand both internal and external factors that influence success.

Internal Factors External Factors
Strengths — internal advantages that help a business compete. Opportunities — external situations that may help the business grow.
Weaknesses — internal limitations that make success more difficult. Threats — external factors that could negatively affect the business.

The Four Elements

Strengths may include: strong brand recognition, loyal customers, unique product features, talented employees, strong financial position, efficient operations. Weaknesses may include: limited funding, low brand awareness, small customer base, lack of experience, outdated technology. Opportunities may include: growing market demand, new technology, partnership opportunities, population growth, changes in consumer preferences. Threats may include: new competitors, economic downturns, rising costs, changing regulations, supply chain disruptions.

Example SWOT Analysis — Student Tutoring Business

  • Strength: Strong academic reputation.
  • Weakness: Limited marketing budget.
  • Opportunity: Increased demand for AP tutoring.
  • Threat: Free online tutoring resources.

Using SWOT to Make Decisions

SWOT analysis becomes valuable when businesses use the results to create strategies: use strengths to take advantage of opportunities, reduce weaknesses before they become major problems, create plans to address threats, and allocate resources more effectively.

Business Canvas Connection

SWOT Response Frame:

One major strength of our business is __________. One weakness is __________. One opportunity is __________. One threat is __________. Based on this SWOT analysis, we should __________.

Key Takeaways

  • SWOT analysis evaluates Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats.
  • Strengths and weaknesses are internal factors the business can influence.
  • Opportunities and threats are external factors in the business environment.
  • SWOT analysis becomes valuable when it leads to strategic action.
  • Businesses should use strengths to capture opportunities and create plans to address threats.

  6. Give two examples of business threats.
  7. How should a business use SWOT findings to make decisions?

Lesson 4.5: Porter's Five Forces

Competitive Rivalry, New Entrants, Substitutes, Customer Power, and Supplier Power

Why This Lesson Matters

A business needs to understand the competitive pressure around it before making major decisions. Porter's Five Forces helps businesses evaluate how difficult it may be to compete in a market. A market may look attractive at first, but strong competition, powerful suppliers, or easy substitutes can make it hard to earn profit.

Essential Question

How can businesses use Porter's Five Forces to evaluate market competitiveness?

Porter's Five Forces Summary

Force Main Question Stronger When…
Competitive Rivalry How intense is competition among current businesses? Many direct competitors; similar products; easy price comparison.
Threat of New Entrants How easy is it for new businesses to enter? Barriers to entry are low — few startup costs, no patents, no regulations.
Threat of Substitutes How easily can customers meet the same need another way? Many alternatives that are cheaper, easier, or better.
Customer Power How much power do customers have to demand lower prices or better value? Customers have many options and can easily switch brands.
Supplier Power How much power do suppliers have to raise costs or limit access? Few suppliers; rare resources; expensive to switch suppliers.

Key Concepts Within the Five Forces

Barriers to entry are obstacles that make it harder for new businesses to compete — high startup costs, patents, government regulations, strong brand loyalty, limited supplier access, or specialized technology. Starting a lemonade stand has low barriers; starting an airline has high barriers.

Switching costs are the money, time, effort, or emotional cost involved in changing from one product or brand to another. If it takes two seconds to switch snack brands, customer power is high. If switching software requires retraining employees and migrating data, customer power is lower.

Business Example

A student coffee cart might face strong competitive rivalry from nearby coffee shops and school vending machines, and high supplier power if it depends on one local supplier for coffee beans. However, it may have an advantage through faster service, lower prices, and a convenient campus location — a combination competitors can't easily match.

Market Attractiveness

A market is usually more attractive when competitive rivalry is low, new entrants face barriers, substitutes are limited, customers have lower switching power, and suppliers have limited pricing power. When these forces are strong, the market is harder to compete in and less attractive.

Business Canvas Connection

Porter's Five Forces Response Frame:

The strongest competitive force affecting our business is __________ because __________. This makes the market __________. To respond, our business should __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Porter's Five Forces helps businesses evaluate competition and market attractiveness.
  • Competitive rivalry looks at competition among existing businesses.
  • Threat of new entrants looks at how easy it is for new businesses to enter.
  • Threat of substitutes looks at alternative ways customers can meet the same need.
  • Customer power looks at buyers' ability to demand lower prices or better value.
  • Supplier power looks at suppliers' ability to raise input costs.
  • Stronger forces usually make a market less attractive.

Lesson 4.6: Competitive Advantage and Strategy

Strategic Recommendations, Internal Capabilities, Competitive Position, and Final Business Decisions

Why This Lesson Matters

Strategic analysis only matters if it leads to a decision. SWOT analysis and Porter's Five Forces help businesses understand their situation. Strategy is what the business does with that information.

Essential Question

How do businesses use strategic analysis to make better decisions and strengthen competitive advantage?

What Is Strategy?

Strategy is a plan for achieving goals and competing successfully. It explains what the business will focus on, how it will compete, what resources it will use, what trade-offs it will accept, and how it will respond to risks. Strategy should connect to evidence — a business should not make decisions only because an idea sounds exciting.

Connecting SWOT to Strategy

SWOT Finding Strategic Question Possible Action
Strength How can we use this advantage? Make it central to branding or operations.
Weakness How can we reduce this limitation? Add training, revise process, or seek support.
Opportunity How can we use this external trend? Adjust product, market, channel, or promotion.
Threat How can we prepare for this risk? Create backup plans or change positioning.

Connecting Porter's Five Forces to Strategy

Strong Force Possible Strategic Response
Strong competitive rivalry Differentiate the product or improve customer experience.
High threat of new entrants Build brand loyalty or create barriers to entry.
High threat of substitutes Show why the product solves the problem better than alternatives.
High customer power Increase switching costs or improve loyalty.
High supplier power Find alternate suppliers or negotiate better terms.

Strategic Recommendation

A strategic recommendation is a clear action a business should take based on evidence. It should include: the decision, the evidence supporting it, the expected benefit, the trade-off or risk, and the next step.

Weak: We should advertise more.

Stronger: We should focus our marketing on student athletes through team group chats and after-school pickup posters because our research shows they have the strongest need and the fastest access to our product. The risk is that our market may be too narrow, so we will track repeat purchases and customer referrals.

Business Example

A tutoring startup completes SWOT and Porter's Five Forces. The team identifies its strength as peer tutoring, its weakness as low brand awareness, its opportunity as increased AP course enrollment, and its biggest threat as free online tutoring. A strong strategy is not "get more customers" — it is to offer low-cost, school-specific AP review sessions that free online services cannot personalize.

Business Canvas Connection

Strategic Recommendation Frame:

Based on our SWOT analysis and Porter's Five Forces analysis, our business should __________. The strongest evidence supporting this decision is __________. The main risk is __________. Our next step should be __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Strategy is a plan for achieving goals and competing successfully.
  • Competitive advantage helps a business outperform rivals.
  • SWOT analysis helps businesses turn findings into action.
  • Porter's Five Forces helps businesses respond to competitive pressure.
  • A strategic recommendation should be specific, evidence-based, and realistic.
  • The final Business Canvas should show how evidence shaped the business strategy.

  4. How should a business respond to a strong threat of substitutes?

Lesson 4.7: Professional Business Communication and Pitching

Presenting Ideas, Persuading Stakeholders, and Communicating Recommendations

Why This Lesson Matters

A great business idea has little value if nobody understands it. Entrepreneurs constantly communicate with customers, employees, suppliers, investors, lenders, and business partners. Strong communication helps businesses gain support, attract customers, secure funding, and build trust. The ability to clearly explain an idea is one of the most important skills an entrepreneur can develop.

Essential Question

How do entrepreneurs communicate business ideas effectively to different audiences?

Knowing Your Audience

Different audiences care about different information. Business communication should always be clear, accurate, professional, organized, audience-focused, and evidence-based.

Audience Primary Concern
Customers How the product solves a problem
Investors Profitability and growth potential
Lenders Ability to repay loans
Employees Roles, expectations, and goals
Partners Mutual benefits and collaboration

Elements of an Effective Business Pitch

A strong pitch should answer: (1) What problem exists? (2) Who experiences the problem? (3) What solution does the business provide? (4) Why is the solution valuable? (5) How does the business make money? (6) What evidence supports the idea? (7) Why is the business likely to succeed? Successful pitches rely on evidence — survey results, market research, revenue projections, competitive analysis, and hypothesis testing results all increase credibility.

Presentation Design and Speaking Skills

Effective presentations use limited text, include relevant visuals, highlight important data, and maintain consistent formatting. Slides should support the speaker, not replace them. Professional speaking means: speak clearly and confidently, maintain eye contact, use professional language, avoid reading directly from slides, support claims with evidence, and answer questions respectfully.

Business Development Project Connection

Your final presentation should communicate how your business solves a problem, creates value, generates revenue, and competes successfully. The presentation should not simply describe the business — it should persuade stakeholders that the business idea is worth supporting.

Key Takeaways

  • Strong communication is essential for business success.
  • Different audiences care about different information.
  • Effective pitches explain problems, solutions, value, and evidence.
  • Evidence increases credibility and helps stakeholders trust the recommendation.
  • Professional presentations focus on clarity and persuasion, not volume of text.
