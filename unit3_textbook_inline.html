Lesson 3.1: Saving for Future Purchases

Savings Goals, Opportunity Cost, Interest, Compound Interest, and Time Value of Money

Why This Lesson Matters

Saving money gives people options. It helps individuals prepare for future purchases, handle emergencies, avoid unnecessary debt, and work toward long-term goals. Businesses also need saving and planning — entrepreneurs often need money for equipment, supplies, marketing, technology, permits, and unexpected costs.

Essential Question

How does saving money help individuals and businesses prepare for future goals?

What Is Saving?

Saving means setting aside money for future use instead of spending it immediately. Saving is not about never spending — it is about deciding when spending makes sense and when money should be kept for a future goal.

Short-Term, Medium-Term, and Long-Term Goals

A financial goal is something a person wants to accomplish with money.

Goal Type Time Frame Example
Short-Term Goal Less than one year Saving for headphones, clothes, or a school event.
Medium-Term Goal One to five years Saving for a car, certification program, or business equipment.
Long-Term Goal More than five years Saving for college, a home, retirement, or long-term business growth.

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost is the value of the next-best option you give up when making a choice. Every financial decision has an opportunity cost. Spending $40 on food delivery means that $40 is no longer available for gas, savings, a concert ticket, or business supplies. Opportunity cost does not mean spending is bad — it means you should understand what you are giving up.

Real-World Example: Starting a Business

A student has $100. They could spend it on clothes or save it to buy supplies for a small sticker business. If they spend on clothes, the opportunity cost is the chance to start the business sooner. If they save for the business, the opportunity cost is giving up the clothes now.

Savings Accounts

A savings account is a bank or credit union account used to store money and earn interest. They typically provide safety, easy access to money, interest earnings, and separation from everyday spending. They are designed for safety and access, not high investment returns.

Simple Interest vs. Compound Interest

Simple interest is calculated only on the original amount. If you save $100 at 5% simple interest for one year, you earn $5 — leaving you with $105.

Compound interest means earning interest on both the original money and the interest already earned. This can grow money faster over time. The longer money stays saved, the more compounding can matter.

Time Value of Money

The time value of money is the idea that money available now is usually worth more than the same amount later because it can earn interest or be invested. $100 today can be saved, invested, or used to start a business. This is why starting to save early matters — time gives money more opportunity to grow.

Business Canvas Connection

Personal saving connects to business finance because entrepreneurs often use savings to fund early business expenses.

Savings Reflection Frame:

One financial goal connected to my business idea is __________. Saving helps with this goal because __________. One opportunity cost of saving for this goal is __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Saving means setting aside money for future use.
  • Financial goals may be short-term, medium-term, or long-term.
  • Opportunity cost is what you give up when choosing one option over another.
  • Interest is money paid for the use of money.
  • Compound interest means earning interest on interest already earned.
  • The time value of money explains why money now can be more valuable than money later.
  • Entrepreneurs often need savings or startup funds before launching a business.

Lesson 3.2: Borrowing, Credit, and Debt

Loans, Credit Scores, Interest, Debt, Risk, and Responsible Borrowing

Why This Lesson Matters

Borrowing money can help people reach goals sooner, but it also creates responsibility. Credit can help someone buy a car, attend college, start a business, or handle an emergency. Poor borrowing decisions can lead to high debt, damaged credit, and financial stress. Entrepreneurs also use borrowing to purchase equipment, rent space, buy inventory, or expand operations.

Essential Question

How can borrowing and credit help or hurt personal and business financial decisions?

Borrowing, Credit, and Debt

Borrowing means receiving money now with a promise to repay it later. Credit is the ability to borrow money or access goods and services with the agreement to pay later. Debt is money owed to another person, lender, or organization. Debt usually must be repaid with interest.

Debt can be manageable when payments are affordable and the borrowed money supports a meaningful goal. Debt becomes dangerous when payments are too high, interest grows quickly, or borrowing is used to cover regular spending without a plan.

Credit Score

A credit score is a number that represents how risky it may be to lend money to a person. Lenders use credit scores to decide whether to approve a loan, what interest rate to charge, and what credit limit to offer. A higher score can lead to lower interest rates; a lower score can make borrowing harder or more expensive.

Factors That Affect Credit

Factor What It Means
Payment History Whether bills are paid on time.
Amounts Owed How much debt is currently being used.
Length of Credit History How long accounts have been open.
New Credit How often new accounts are opened.
Credit Mix Different types of credit accounts held.

Real-World Example: Car Loan

Two people apply for the same car loan. One has strong credit and receives a lower interest rate. The other has weak credit and receives a higher rate. They may buy the same car, but the person with the higher interest rate will pay more over time.

Good Debt vs. Risky Debt

Debt Type Possible Example Why It May Help or Hurt
Potentially Helpful Debt Student loan for a program that leads to higher income. May support future earning power if the cost is reasonable.
Risky Debt Credit card debt from regular spending with no repayment plan. High interest can grow quickly and create financial stress.

Responsible Borrowing

Before borrowing, people should ask: Why am I borrowing? How much do I need? What is the interest rate? What is the total cost? Can I afford the monthly payment? What happens if my income changes? Is there a better alternative? Borrowing should support a plan, not replace one.

Business Canvas Connection

Borrowing Reflection Frame:

Borrowing could help my business by __________. However, it could create risk because __________. Before borrowing, I would need to know __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Borrowing means receiving money now and promising to repay it later.
  • Credit is the ability to borrow or access goods and services with payment later.
  • Debt is money owed to a lender or organization.
  • Interest is the cost of borrowing money.
  • A credit score helps lenders judge borrowing risk.
  • Debt can help when it is useful, affordable, and planned.
  • Businesses may borrow to start or grow, but borrowing creates repayment risk.

Lesson 3.3: Startup Costs

Estimating Expenses, Financial Planning, Risk Management, and Business Readiness

Why This Lesson Matters

Many business ideas fail because owners underestimate how much money they need before opening. A business may have strong marketing, a great product, and interested customers, but without enough money to operate, it can quickly run into problems.

Essential Question

How do entrepreneurs estimate the money needed to start a business?

What Are Startup Costs?

Startup costs are the expenses required to launch a business before regular operations begin. These expenses occur before the business starts generating consistent revenue. Startup costs vary widely — a tutoring service may require very little money, while a restaurant may require hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Common Startup Costs

Equipment, inventory, supplies, technology, marketing materials, website development, permits and licenses, insurance, legal fees, rent deposits, training, and professional services.

One-Time vs. Ongoing Costs

One-Time Costs Ongoing Costs
Business registration Rent
Initial equipment purchase Utilities
Logo design Payroll
Initial inventory Advertising

Estimating Startup Costs

Entrepreneurs should estimate startup costs using research rather than guesses. Good estimates come from supplier websites, industry reports, competitor research, vendor quotes, professional advice, and market research. Businesses often create a startup budget before launching.

Contingency Planning

Unexpected expenses frequently appear after launch — equipment repairs, software subscriptions, shipping increases, inventory shortages. Contingency funds are extra money set aside for unexpected expenses. Businesses often add a percentage above estimated startup costs to prepare for surprises.

Business Example

A student-operated custom t-shirt business estimates startup costs of $750. After researching equipment, website fees, supplies, and marketing, they add a 10% contingency amount. The final startup budget becomes $825.

Business Canvas Connection

Startup Cost Planning Frame:

Our business requires __________ dollars to start. The largest startup expenses include __________. We estimated these costs by __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Startup costs are expenses required before business operations begin.
  • Startup costs vary by business type.
  • Research improves financial estimates.
  • Businesses should distinguish between one-time and ongoing costs.
  • Contingency planning helps prepare for unexpected expenses.
  • Startup budgets help entrepreneurs make informed decisions.

Check Your Understanding

  3. Give two examples of ongoing business costs.
  4. Why should entrepreneurs research their cost estimates?
  5. What is a contingency fund?
  6. Why is underestimating startup costs dangerous?

Lesson 3.4: Sources of Financial Capital

Debt Financing, Equity Financing, Investors, Loans, Crowdfunding, and Financial Risk

Why This Lesson Matters

Most businesses need money before they can earn money. Entrepreneurs must decide where startup funding will come from and what trade-offs accompany each option.

Essential Question

How do entrepreneurs choose the best source of financial capital?

What Is Financial Capital?

Financial capital is money available for business activities — used to purchase equipment, buy inventory, hire employees, develop products, market products, and expand operations.

Debt vs. Equity Financing

Type Meaning Advantage Disadvantage
Debt Financing Borrowing money that must be repaid with interest. Business ownership stays unchanged; owners keep control. Repayment required; interest increases costs.
Equity Financing Raising money by giving up part ownership. No loan payments; investors may offer expertise and networks. Owners share control and may share profits.

Common Sources of Financial Capital

Personal savings — no repayment required, but uses the founder's own money. Friends and family — flexible terms, but can strain relationships. Angel investors — individuals who invest their own money in early-stage businesses in exchange for equity, often looking for strong ideas, large market opportunities, capable founders, and growth potential. Venture capital — firms that invest in high-growth businesses, common in technology; involves giving up ownership. Crowdfunding — raising money from many individuals online through donation-based, reward-based, or equity approaches; can also test customer interest before launch. Business grants — free money from government or organizations; competitive but requires no repayment.

Business Example

A startup needs $5,000 to launch. The founders compare three options: use personal savings, take a small business loan, or raise money through crowdfunding. Each option provides funding but creates different risks and trade-offs.

Business Canvas Connection

Financial Capital Planning Frame:

Our business plans to obtain startup funding through __________. We selected this option because __________. The primary advantage is __________. The primary risk is __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Financial capital is money available for business activities.
  • Debt financing requires repayment with interest but ownership stays unchanged.
  • Equity financing involves giving up ownership but requires no loan payments.
  • Angel investors and venture capitalists provide funding in exchange for equity.
  • Crowdfunding can provide funding while also testing market demand.
  • Entrepreneurs must evaluate trade-offs before selecting funding sources.

Lesson 3.5: Revenue, Expenses, and Profit

Revenue Streams, Business Expenses, Profit, Loss, Break-Even Thinking, and Financial Planning

Why This Lesson Matters

A business must understand how money moves in and out. A company can make sales and still lose money if expenses are too high. Entrepreneurs need to estimate revenue, track expenses, and understand profit before making decisions about pricing, hiring, production, marketing, or growth.

Essential Question

How do businesses use revenue, expenses, and profit to evaluate financial performance?

Revenue and Revenue Streams

Revenue is the money a business earns from selling goods or services. A revenue stream is a specific way a business earns money. Revenue alone does not show whether a business is successful — a business can earn a lot of revenue and still lose money if expenses are too high.

Business Revenue Stream Example
Coffee shop Product sales Coffee, pastries, merchandise
Streaming service Subscription fees Monthly customer payments
Social media platform Advertising Businesses pay to show ads
Gym Membership fees Monthly membership dues

Fixed and Variable Expenses

Fixed expenses stay the same even if the business sells more or fewer products — rent, insurance, website hosting, monthly software subscriptions. Variable expenses change depending on how many products or services the business sells — ingredients, packaging, shipping, sales commissions. A snack box business may pay the same monthly website fee, but the cost of snacks and packaging increases as more boxes are sold.

Profit, Loss, and Break-Even

Profit = Revenue − Expenses

If revenue is greater than expenses, the business earns a profit. If expenses are greater than revenue, the business has a loss. Break-even means the business earns enough revenue to cover its expenses, but does not yet earn profit.

Business Example: Break-Even

A custom sticker business spends $200 on supplies, packaging, and marketing. If each sticker pack sells for $5, the business must sell 40 packs to cover the $200 in expenses. After 40 packs, the business begins earning profit — assuming no additional expenses are added.

Profit Example

A student business sells 100 snack boxes for $5 each. Total revenue: 100 × $5 = $500. Total expenses: $320. Profit: $500 − $320 = $180.

Revenue Projection

A revenue projection is an estimate of how much money a business expects to earn in the future, based on number of customers, price per product, sales volume, repeat purchases, market demand, and seasonality. Projections are not guarantees — they are educated estimates that should explain their assumptions.

Business Canvas Connection

Revenue Projection Frame:

Our business expects to sell __________ units at __________ dollars each during __________. This would create estimated revenue of __________. This projection is based on __________.

Example: Our business expects to sell 100 snack boxes at $5 each during the first month. Estimated revenue: $500. This projection is based on survey interest, after-school sports participation, and the number of practice days in the month.

Key Takeaways

  • Revenue is money earned from selling goods or services.
  • A revenue stream is a specific way a business earns money.
  • Fixed expenses stay the same regardless of sales volume.
  • Variable expenses change based on sales or production volume.
  • Profit is revenue minus expenses.
  • A loss occurs when expenses are greater than revenue.
  • Break-even means revenue equals expenses.
  • A revenue projection estimates future revenue based on assumptions.

Lesson 3.6: Financial Statements and Income Statements

Financial Reporting, Income Statements, Revenue, Expenses, Net Income, and Business Performance

Why This Lesson Matters

Business owners need financial information to make decisions — whether the business is earning money, losing money, spending too much, or growing sustainably. Financial statements organize financial information so owners, managers, lenders, and investors can evaluate performance.

Essential Question

How do businesses use income statements to evaluate financial performance?

What Is an Income Statement?

Financial statements are formal reports that summarize a business's financial activities. An income statement is a financial statement that shows revenue, expenses, and profit or loss over a specific period of time. It answers: How much revenue did the business earn? How much did it spend? Did it make a profit or lose money?

Income Statement Structure

Section Meaning Example
Revenue Money earned from sales. $500 from snack box sales.
Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) Direct costs of producing products sold. Snacks, drinks, boxes, labels.
Gross Profit Revenue minus cost of goods sold. Revenue after direct product costs.
Operating Expenses General costs of running the business. Marketing, website, storage, software.
Net Income Final profit after all expenses. Money left after all listed expenses.

Key Formulas

Gross Profit = Revenue − Cost of Goods Sold

Net Income = Gross Profit − Operating Expenses

Sample Income Statement

Item Amount
Revenue $500
Cost of Goods Sold $260
Gross Profit $240
Operating Expenses $90
Net Income $150

This business earned $150 in net income for the period.

Projected Income Statement

A projected income statement estimates future revenue, expenses, and profit. Startups use projections because the business may not have actual financial results yet. Entrepreneurs use projections to plan, persuade lenders or investors, and test whether the business idea is financially realistic.

Business Canvas Connection

Income Statement Reflection Frame:

Our projected income statement shows estimated revenue of __________ and estimated expenses of __________. Based on these estimates, our projected net income is __________. This suggests our business is __________ because __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Financial statements summarize business financial activity.
  • An income statement shows revenue, expenses, and profit or loss over a period of time.
  • Cost of goods sold includes direct product costs.
  • Gross profit equals revenue minus cost of goods sold.
  • Operating expenses are general costs of running the business.
  • Net income is final profit after all expenses.
  • A projected income statement estimates future financial performance.

Lesson 3.7: Ethical Financial Reporting

Accuracy, Transparency, Stakeholders, Financial Decisions, and Business Trust

Why This Lesson Matters

Financial information affects major business decisions. Owners, managers, lenders, investors, employees, and customers may rely on financial reports to understand how a business is performing. If financial information is inaccurate or misleading, people can make poor decisions. A lender may approve a loan the business cannot repay. An investor may put money into a company that is weaker than it appears. Ethical financial reporting protects trust.

Essential Question

Why must businesses report financial information accurately and honestly?

What Is Ethical Financial Reporting?

Ethical financial reporting means presenting financial information honestly, accurately, clearly, and responsibly. It requires businesses to avoid exaggerating revenue, hiding expenses, misleading investors, or manipulating numbers to make the business look stronger than it is. Transparency means financial information is clear, honest, and not intentionally hidden.

Common Financial Reporting Problems

Unethical reporting includes: recording fake sales, hiding expenses, overstating profits, leaving out debt, misclassifying expenses, changing numbers to attract investors, making unsupported projections, and using confusing reports to hide poor performance.

Business Example

A startup wants investors to provide $25,000. To make the business look stronger, the owners exaggerate expected sales and leave out several major expenses. The investors believe the business is more profitable than it really is. That is unethical because the financial information does not show the true risk.

Stakeholders Affected by Financial Reporting

Stakeholder Why Financial Reporting Matters
Owners They need to know if the business is profitable and sustainable.
Managers They use reports to make decisions about pricing, hiring, and growth.
Lenders They need to know if the business can repay borrowed money.
Investors They need accurate information before risking their money.
Employees They may be affected by business growth, cuts, pay, or job security.
Suppliers They may need to know if the business can pay its bills on time.

Strong vs. Weak Financial Projections

Weak Projection Stronger Projection
We will sell 10,000 units because our product is amazing. We estimate 150 monthly sales based on survey interest, target customer size, planned price, and expected repeat purchases.
Expenses will be low. Estimated monthly expenses include $120 for supplies, $40 for packaging, $25 for marketing, and $15 for software.

Ethical Pitching to Lenders and Investors

An ethical pitch should explain the business opportunity clearly, use evidence to support claims, be honest about startup costs, be realistic about sales projections, explain major risks, show how funding will be used, and avoid fake or inflated numbers. Making a business sound attractive is normal — hiding major risks is not.

Business Canvas Connection

Ethical Financial Reporting Frame:

Our financial projection is based on __________. One assumption we made is __________. One risk in our financial plan is __________. To report ethically, we should __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Ethical financial reporting means presenting financial information honestly and accurately.
  • Financial reports affect decisions made by owners, managers, lenders, investors, employees, and suppliers.
  • Transparency means financial information is clear and not intentionally hidden.
  • Unethical reporting includes fake sales, hidden expenses, overstated profits, and unsupported projections.
  • Financial projections should be based on reasonable assumptions and evidence.
  • Entrepreneurial pitches should communicate opportunity without hiding major risks.
