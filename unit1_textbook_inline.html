Lesson 1.1: What Is a Business?

Businesses, Customers, Consumers, Value Creation, and Value Capture

Why This Lesson Matters

Before you can build a business, you need to understand what a business actually does. A business becomes successful when it solves a real customer problem, meets a real need, or satisfies a real want.

Essential Question

How do businesses create value by solving customer problems, meeting needs, and satisfying wants?

What Is a Business?

A business is an organization or entity that produces and distributes goods, services, or both. It can be small, large, local, national, or global — and even though businesses look different, they all share the same basic goal: they provide something people value.

Goods and Services

Term Meaning Examples
Good A physical product that can be touched. Shoes, phones, food, cars, books
Service An activity performed for a customer. Haircuts, tutoring, streaming, delivery, banking

Some businesses provide both. Example: Starbucks sells coffee and food (goods) and also provides mobile ordering and customer service (services).

Real-World Example: Netflix

Netflix does not sell a physical product. It sells access to entertainment. Customers pay for a streaming service that lets them watch shows and movies when they want.

Netflix creates value by making entertainment convenient. It captures value through subscriptions and advertising on some plans.

Customers and Consumers

A customer is the person or business that purchases a good or service. A consumer is the person who uses it. They are sometimes the same — and sometimes different.

Situation Customer Consumer
A parent buys a Happy Meal for their child. Parent Child
A school buys laptops for students. School Students
A student buys headphones for themselves. Student Student

Problems, Needs, and Wants

Problems

A problem is something that causes frustration, inconvenience, wasted time, or difficulty. Many businesses start because someone notices a problem and thinks, "There has to be a better way."

Needs

A need is something people require for daily life, health, safety, or basic functioning — food, water, housing, healthcare, transportation.

Wants

A want is something people desire because it gives comfort, enjoyment, convenience, or status — theme park tickets, streaming subscriptions, specialty coffee. Wants are powerful: many successful businesses sell things people don't technically need but still value.

Think Like a Founder

A smartphone connects to all three:

  • Problem: You need a way to communicate when away from home.
  • Need: You need access to information, safety tools, and communication.
  • Want: You want the newest camera, color, apps, or brand.

Value Creation

Value creation happens when a business provides a product that responds to customers' problems, needs, or wants — in other words, it makes the customer's life better.

Business Problem / Need / Want How It Creates Value
Amazon Convenient shopping Large selection and fast delivery
Uber Transportation without owning a car Connects riders with drivers via app
DoorDash Food without leaving home Delivers restaurant food to customers
Local tutoring service Help with difficult topics Individual support and practice

Value Capture

Value capture happens when a business earns revenue from the value it creates. Methods include product sales, service fees, subscriptions, advertising, and memberships.

Common Business Problem

A business can create value and still fail. Imagine students build an app that classmates love — but nobody pays for it. The app creates value, but the business does not capture value. That makes it hard to continue.

Problem-Solution Fit

Problem-solution fit happens when a product actually addresses a real customer problem, need, or want.

Weak Thinking Stronger Thinking
I want to make an app. Students struggle to track assignments across multiple platforms.
I want to sell shirts. Students want affordable spirit wear that feels modern and comfortable.
I want to open a cafe. Students and staff need quick breakfast options before school.

Business Canvas Connection

Your first Business Canvas step is identifying a customer problem, need, or want. Use this format:

Template:

I believe that [target customer] struggles with [specific problem] because [reason or evidence].

Example: I believe that high school students struggle to find affordable and healthy lunch options because most nearby food options are expensive, unhealthy, or unavailable during the school day.

Key Takeaways

  • A business produces and distributes goods, services, or both.
  • A customer buys the product; a consumer uses it.
  • Businesses address customer problems, needs, and wants.
  • Value creation means the business improves the customer's life.
  • Value capture means the business earns revenue from the value it creates.
  • Problem-solution fit means a product actually addresses a real customer need.

Lesson 1.2: Markets and Competitive Advantage

Markets, Buyers, Sellers, Competition, and Business Positioning

Why This Lesson Matters

A business does not operate alone. Even with a great product, it must answer: Why would customers choose us instead of someone else? That question is at the center of competitive advantage.

Essential Question

How do businesses compete in markets and convince customers to choose their products?

What Is a Market?

A market is any physical or virtual space where sellers and buyers interact. Markets can be local, regional, national, global, or virtual. They exist because sellers want to sell and buyers want products that solve problems, meet needs, or satisfy wants.

Buyers and Sellers

Group What They Want Example
Sellers Higher prices and enough sales to earn profit. A shoe company wants to sell sneakers at a profitable price.
Buyers Lower prices and products that feel worth the money. A student wants good shoes without overpaying.

Competition

Competition happens when multiple businesses try to serve the same customers. It gives customers choices. For example, someone wanting coffee may choose Starbucks, Dunkin', Wawa, a local shop, or homemade coffee.

Real-World Example: Coffee Competition

Starbucks competes through brand, mobile ordering, and seasonal drinks. Dunkin' competes through lower prices and speed. A local coffee shop competes through atmosphere, community, and specialty drinks. They all sell coffee, but don't compete in exactly the same way.

Competitive Advantage

Competitive advantage is the ability of a business to outperform rivals in the same market. Sources include lower prices, higher quality, better service, unique features, convenience, speed, brand reputation, and location. A business doesn't need to be better at everything — it needs to be better at something customers care about.

Differentiation

Differentiation means making a product different in a way customers value — through design, features, brand identity, materials, customer experience, or personalization. Apple competes through design, brand loyalty, and the way its products work together — not necessarily the cheapest price.

Cost Advantage

A business can also compete by producing or delivering at a lower cost than rivals. Walmart competes through low prices, large-scale purchasing, and efficient operations.

Barriers to Entry

A barrier to entry is something that makes it hard for new businesses to enter a market. Examples include high startup costs, patents, government regulations, exclusive supplier relationships, strong brand loyalty, and expensive technology. Starting a lawn care business is much easier than starting an airline.

Monopoly

A monopoly exists when one business is the only provider in a market. Even dominant companies must pay attention to customers — if customers find alternatives, the business can lose power.

Business Canvas Connection

Your Business Canvas must explain how your business will compete. Be able to answer: Who are our competitors? What alternatives do customers use? Why would customers choose us?

Competitive Advantage Statement Template:

Our business will compete by __________ because our target customers value __________.

Example: Our business will compete by offering affordable, healthy grab-and-go lunches because our target customers value speed, price, and healthier choices during the school day.

Key Takeaways

  • A market is where buyers and sellers interact.
  • Competition gives customers choices.
  • Competitive advantage gives customers a reason to choose one business over another.
  • Businesses can compete through price, quality, service, brand, or convenience.
  • Barriers to entry make it harder for new businesses to compete.

  2. What is the difference between a buyer and a seller?
  3. What is competition?
  4. What is competitive advantage?
  5. Give one example of a business that competes through low prices.
  6. Give one example of a business that competes through differentiation.
  7. What is a barrier to entry?
  8. Why is it important for a business to understand its competitors?
  9. Write a competitive advantage statement for a business idea you might create.

Lesson 1.3: PESTEL Factors and the Business Environment

Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, and Legal Factors

Why This Lesson Matters

Outside factors can affect whether a business idea works. Business owners cannot control everything around them, but they can study these factors and make better decisions.

Essential Question

How do outside factors affect whether a business idea is attractive, risky, or realistic?

What Is PESTEL?

PESTEL is a framework businesses use to study external factors that can affect a market. It stands for Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, and Legal. These factors help evaluate opportunity and risk.

Political Factors

Political factors are government policies and political conditions that affect business activity — taxes, trade rules, grants, mandates, bans, and political stability.

Example: A city offering grants to small businesses makes the market more attractive. A ban on certain plastic containers forces a restaurant to change packaging.

Economic Factors

Economic factors are conditions that affect how customers and businesses spend money — inflation, interest rates, unemployment, household income, and cost of supplies.

Example: If inflation rises, ingredients cost more, and a bakery may need to raise prices. If unemployment rises, customers may cut spending on restaurants and entertainment.

Social Factors

Social factors are trends in society, culture, demographics, and customer behavior — age trends, lifestyle changes, health preferences, cultural norms, and consumer values.

Example: More customers caring about fitness may make a smoothie business more attractive. A preference for short-form video may shift advertising strategy toward TikTok.

Technological Factors

Technological factors are tools, systems, and innovations that affect how businesses produce, sell, and operate — internet access, mobile apps, AI, automation, and payment systems.

Example: Online ordering helps a small business reach more customers. Video calls allow a tutoring business to serve students virtually.

Environmental Factors

Environmental factors are natural conditions and concerns — weather, climate, natural disasters, resource access, waste management, and customer concern about sustainability.

Example: A beach rental business depends on weather. A reusable water bottle company benefits from customer concern about plastic waste.

Legal Factors

Legal factors are laws, rules, and regulations — employment laws, health and safety rules, consumer protection laws, intellectual property rules, food safety, and licensing requirements.

Example: A food business may need permits and inspections. A clothing company cannot legally copy another company's trademarked logo.

Real-World Example: Starting a Local Food Business

  • Political: Local government may support youth entrepreneurship.
  • Economic: Students may have limited spending money.
  • Social: Students may want quick, affordable, trendy food.
  • Technological: Online ordering could make pickup faster.
  • Environmental: Customers may prefer eco-friendly packaging.
  • Legal: Food safety rules and permits may apply.

Applying PESTEL Well

PESTEL Factor Weak Example Stronger Example
Economic Prices are high. Higher ingredient prices may force our lunch business to increase prices or reduce portion sizes.
Social People like healthy food. More students are interested in fitness, which may increase demand for healthier lunch options.
Legal There are rules. Food safety rules may require permits, inspections, and approved preparation areas before we can sell food.

Business Canvas Connection

PESTEL Sentence Template:

The __________ factor affects our business because __________. This creates a(n) __________ because __________.

Example: The economic factor affects our business because students may have limited spending money. This creates a risk because our product must stay affordable to attract repeat customers.

Key Takeaways

  • PESTEL helps businesses study external factors.
  • Political: policies, taxes, government support.
  • Economic: inflation, income, unemployment, spending patterns.
  • Social: culture, demographics, lifestyle trends, consumer values.
  • Technological: tools, systems, automation, innovation.
  • Environmental: weather, natural resources, sustainability concerns.
  • Legal: laws, permits, safety rules, regulations.
  • PESTEL helps evaluate market attractiveness and risk.

  9. What does business viability mean?

Lesson 1.4: How Do Business Ideas Originate?

Entrepreneurship, Customer Discovery, Risk, Validation, and Problem-Solution Fit

Why This Lesson Matters

Business ideas do not usually appear out of nowhere. Most strong ideas come from observing problems, listening to customers, researching markets, and testing possible solutions. Entrepreneurs often start with an imperfect idea, test it, receive feedback, and keep improving.

Essential Question

How do entrepreneurs identify, test, and improve business ideas before launching them?

What Is an Entrepreneur?

An entrepreneur is a person who develops a new business and accepts the risks and potential rewards of that business. Strong entrepreneurs identify opportunities, solve problems, make decisions, manage resources, and adapt when things go wrong.

Where Business Ideas Come From

Business ideas often come from noticing a gap between what people experience and what they want or need. Strategies include:

  • Observation: Watching how people behave and noticing frustrations.
  • Interviews: Asking potential customers about their experiences.
  • Surveys: Collecting responses from a larger group.
  • Market research: Studying trends, competitors, and customer behavior.
  • Experimentation: Testing possible solutions and learning from results.

Real-World Example: DoorDash

DoorDash began by noticing that many restaurants wanted delivery but lacked drivers, while customers wanted food delivered. The idea came from observing a real gap in the market.

Interviews

Good interviews do not start with "Do you like my idea?" — people may say yes just to be polite. Better questions focus on the customer's actual experience.

Weak Question Stronger Question
Would you buy my app? When was the last time you missed an assignment deadline?
Do you like healthy food? What do you usually eat for lunch during the school week?
Would you use this product? How do you currently solve this problem?

Surveys

Strong survey questions are clear, specific, neutral, and measurable.

Weak: Don't you agree that school lunch needs better healthy options?

Stronger: How many times per week do you purchase lunch from outside the cafeteria?

Risk in Entrepreneurship

Risk means the possibility that something may not go as expected. Entrepreneurs may risk money, time, reputation, opportunity cost, and human resources. There is no guarantee customers will buy — that is why validation matters.

Think Like a Founder

Imagine spending $500 creating custom water bottles, assuming everyone will want one. Then you learn students find the design boring and the price too high. Testing design and price before ordering inventory would have prevented this.

Design Thinking

Design thinking is a problem-solving process that helps entrepreneurs understand customers and create better solutions. The process: (1) Observe and identify a customer problem. (2) Validate that the problem is real. (3) Develop a possible solution. (4) Create a simple version. (5) Gather feedback. (6) Revise the idea.

Hypothesis Testing

A hypothesis is an educated guess that can be tested. Instead of "Everyone will want this," stronger thinking says: "We believe a specific customer group has a specific problem, and we will test that assumption."

Hypothesis Template:

We believe that __________ experiences __________ because __________.

Example: We believe that students who stay after school for clubs and sports struggle to find affordable snacks because the cafeteria is closed and nearby options are expensive.

Minimum Viable Product

A minimum viable product (MVP) is the simplest version of a product that can be shown to customers for feedback. It could be a sketch, a product description, a sample menu, a mock website, or a short demo. The goal is learning, not proving perfection.

Real-World Example: Testing Before Building

Want to create a planner app? You don't need to build the full app first. Sketch the screens on paper, show students how it would work, and ask what they like or dislike. That simple sketch serves as an MVP.

Business Canvas Connection

Business Canvas Response Frame:

We identified __________ as our target customer. Our research showed that this customer struggles with __________. Based on our findings, our product idea is __________ because __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Entrepreneurs develop new businesses and accept risk.
  • Business ideas often come from observation, interviews, surveys, research, and experimentation.
  • Validation means gathering evidence that a problem or solution is real.
  • Hypothesis testing helps businesses test assumptions.
  • An MVP is the simplest version of a product used to collect feedback.
  • Strong entrepreneurs revise ideas based on evidence.

  6. What is a hypothesis?
  7. Why should entrepreneurs test hypotheses?

  10. Why should entrepreneurs revise ideas based on customer feedback?

Lesson 1.5: Vision

Core Values, Core Competencies, Vision Statements, and Mission Statements

Why This Lesson Matters

A business needs more than a product idea. It needs direction — to decide what opportunities to pursue, what customers to serve, what values to protect, and what kind of organization to build. Without direction, a business can become scattered.

Essential Question

How do values, strengths, mission, and vision guide business decisions?

Core Values

Core values are the beliefs and principles that guide how a person or business acts. They answer: What do we stand for? Strong businesses use values to guide decisions — not just as words on a poster.

Real-World Example: Customer Service as a Value

If reliability is truly a core value, the business shows up on time, communicates delays, completes repairs correctly, and follows up with customers. If the business says it values reliability but constantly misses appointments, the value is not being practiced.

Core Competencies

Core competencies are the skills, capabilities, and strengths that help a business perform well. They answer: What are we good at? A business should make decisions that match its strengths.

Values vs. Competencies

Concept Question It Answers Example
Core Values What do we believe? We value transparency and honesty.
Core Competencies What are we good at? We are skilled at fast delivery and customer support.

Vision Statement

A vision statement describes what a business hopes to become or achieve in the future. It answers: Where are we going?

Weak: We want to be good.

Stronger: We aim to make healthy, affordable food accessible to students during and after the school day.

Mission Statement

A mission statement explains what a business does and how it plans to achieve its goals. It answers: What do we do, who do we serve, and how?

Weak: We sell food.

Stronger: We provide affordable, nutritious grab-and-go snacks for student athletes who need quick food before practice.

Mission vs. Vision

Statement Focus Question Answered
Mission Present action What do we do right now?
Vision Future direction What do we want to become?

Businesses, Social Enterprises, and Nonprofits

A social enterprise is a business that seeks to earn profit while also addressing a social problem. A nonprofit organization exists to serve a public good rather than generate profit — surplus funds are reinvested into the organization, not distributed to owners.

Business Canvas Connection

Mission Statement Template: Our mission is to provide __________ for __________ by __________.

Vision Statement Template: Our vision is to become __________ by helping __________.

Example Mission: Our mission is to provide affordable, nutritious snack boxes for student athletes by offering quick pickup before after-school practices.

Example Vision: Our vision is to become the most trusted student-run snack provider by helping athletes stay energized and prepared after school.

Key Takeaways

  • Core values are beliefs that guide behavior.
  • Core competencies are skills that help a business compete.
  • A vision statement explains what the business hopes to become.
  • A mission statement explains what the business does and how it serves customers.
  • Mission is about current purpose; vision is about future direction.

  3. How are values different from competencies?
  4. What is a vision statement?
  5. What is a mission statement?
  6. How are mission and vision statements different?

Lesson 1.6: Business Ethics

Ethical Behavior, Stakeholders, Ethical Dilemmas, and Business Responsibility

Why This Lesson Matters

Business decisions affect people — customers, employees, owners, suppliers, communities, and the environment. Ethics helps business leaders decide what they should do, not just what they can do.

Essential Question

How should businesses make decisions when profit, values, and stakeholder interests conflict?

What Is Business Ethics?

Business ethics refers to the principles that guide right and wrong behavior in business. Ethical decisions build trust. Unethical decisions can damage customers, employees, communities, and the business itself.

How Businesses Encourage Ethical Behavior

  • Codes of conduct: written rules for expected behavior
  • Ethics training: teaching employees how to respond to ethical issues
  • Internal consequences: penalties for unethical actions
  • Leadership modeling: leaders acting ethically themselves
  • Reporting systems: safe ways for employees to report concerns

Real-World Example: Trust and Brand Loyalty

Two companies sell the same product at the same price. One is known for fair treatment and honest advertising. The other is known for misleading claims and poor working conditions. Many customers will choose the trustworthy company — even if the product is identical.

Stakeholders

A stakeholder is a person or group affected by a business decision.

Type Definition Examples
Internal People inside the business. Owners, managers, employees, board members
External People or groups outside the business affected by its decisions. Customers, suppliers, government, community members, investors

Ethical Dilemmas

An ethical dilemma happens when a decision involves a conflict between values, goals, or stakeholder interests. There may not be one perfect answer. Examples:

  • Lower prices through cheaper materials — but quality suffers.
  • Increase profit by cutting employee hours — but workers struggle financially.
  • Collect customer data to improve marketing — but customers may be concerned about privacy.

Business Canvas Connection

Ethics Reflection Frame:

One ethical issue our business may face is __________. This affects __________ because __________. To respond responsibly, our business should __________.

Example: One ethical issue our business may face is advertising our snack boxes as healthy. This affects customers because misleading health claims could cause poor food choices. To respond responsibly, our business should list ingredients clearly and avoid exaggerating nutrition claims.

Key Takeaways

  • Business ethics guides right and wrong behavior in business.
  • Ethical behavior can build trust, loyalty, and reputation.
  • Stakeholders are people or groups affected by business decisions.
  • Internal stakeholders are inside the business; external are outside.
  • Ethical dilemmas involve conflicts between values, goals, or stakeholder interests.
  • Leaders should consider benefits, harms, values, and long-term trust.

Check Your Understanding

  3. Why can ethical behavior help a business succeed?
  4. What is a stakeholder?
  5. What is the difference between internal and external stakeholders?
  6. What is an ethical dilemma?

  8. How can a business encourage employees to act ethically?
  9. Choose a business idea and identify one ethical issue it could face.

Lesson 1.7: Organization, Roles, and Responsibilities

Business Ownership, Team Roles, Departments, and Organizational Structure

Why This Lesson Matters

A business idea is only the beginning. Someone has to make the product, market it, manage money, serve customers, and make decisions. As a business grows, owners must organize work, divide responsibilities, and choose the best structure.

Essential Question

How do businesses organize people, roles, and responsibilities to operate effectively?

Major Types of Business Organization

Type Basic Meaning Common Strength Common Weakness
Sole Proprietorship Owned by one person. Simple to start; owner keeps control. Owner has personal liability for debts.
Partnership Owned by two or more people. Partners can share skills, money, and work. Partners may disagree; personal liability.
LLC Protects owners from personal liability. Liability protection and flexibility. More paperwork and state filing costs.
Corporation A separate legal entity owned by shareholders. Can raise large amounts of money. Owners give up some control.

Real-World Example: Ownership and Growth

A student-run tutoring business might begin as a simple solo business. If it adds partners, hires tutors, expands to multiple schools, or accepts investors, the structure may need to change. The best structure depends on risk, control, funding needs, and growth goals.

Specialized Departments

Department Main Responsibilities Example Task
Sales and Marketing Attract customers, conduct research, promote products. Create a social media campaign.
Research and Development Develop and improve products. Test a new snack flavor.
Operations Manage production, delivery, inventory, daily processes. Plan how orders are prepared and delivered.
Finance and Accounting Track money, budgets, costs, financial data. Calculate startup costs and profit.
Human Resources Hire, train, support, and manage employees. Write a job description for a team member.

Leadership vs. Management

Leadership sets direction, motivates people, and guides toward goals. Management plans, organizes, coordinates, and evaluates work. A business needs both — a leader inspires the vision, a manager makes sure daily work gets done.

Outsourcing

Outsourcing means hiring another business to handle a function or task — useful when a business lacks a specific skill, another company can do it more efficiently, or the task is not central to the business's core strengths.

Business Canvas Connection

Organization Response Frame:

Our business will need roles in __________, __________, and __________ because __________. Team responsibilities will be divided based on __________.

Key Takeaways

  • Business structures include sole proprietorships, partnerships, LLCs, and corporations.
  • Small business owners often perform many roles simultaneously.
  • Large businesses divide work into specialized departments.
  • Leadership sets direction; management organizes and evaluates work.
  • Outsourcing allows businesses to hire outside help for specific tasks.

Check Your Understanding

  1. What is a sole proprietorship?
  2. What is a partnership?
  3. What is an LLC?
  4. What is a corporation?
  5. Why might a business owner choose an LLC instead of a sole proprietorship?
  6. Why do large businesses use specialized departments?
  7. What is the difference between leadership and management?
  8. What is outsourcing?
  9. Choose a business idea and list three roles the business would need.

Lesson 1.8: Supply Chains

Inputs, Production, Suppliers, Distribution, Logistics, and Risk

Why This Lesson Matters

A business idea is not complete until the business can explain how the product will actually reach the customer. Even a great product can fail if the business cannot get supplies, produce the product, manage inventory, or handle delays. Supply chains connect ideas to real products and services.

Essential Question

How do businesses turn resources into products and deliver value to customers?

What Is a Supply Chain?

A supply chain is the full system of people, businesses, resources, activities, and information involved in creating and delivering a product to customers. For a service business, the supply chain may look different, but the business still needs people, tools, systems, and processes to deliver value.

Inputs

Inputs are the resources a business needs to create a product or service — raw materials, ingredients, equipment, technology, labor, packaging, and energy. A smoothie business needs fruit, cups, lids, blenders, workers, recipes, and payment processing.

Suppliers

Suppliers provide the materials, products, or services a business needs to operate. Choosing suppliers matters because they affect cost, quality, speed, reliability, ethics, and environmental impact. A cheap supplier may lower costs, but poor quality or late deliveries can hurt the business.

Production

Production is the process of turning inputs into goods or services. Businesses need clear production processes so work can be done consistently — whether preparing food, designing a product, writing software, packaging orders, or providing tutoring sessions.

Distribution

Distribution is how the product gets from the business to the customer — through physical stores, online ordering, delivery apps, school pickup locations, or shipping companies. A product may be excellent, but if customers cannot easily get it, the business may struggle.

Logistics

Logistics refers to planning and managing the movement of goods, services, and information — including sourcing, inventory levels, order delivery, production timing, and tracking. Good logistics reduces waste, saves time, lowers costs, and improves customer satisfaction.

Real-World Example: A School Snack Box Business

  • Inputs: snacks, boxes, labels, payment app, storage space
  • Suppliers: grocery store, wholesale snack supplier, packaging supplier
  • Production: assembling snack boxes before pickup
  • Distribution: pickup table near the gym
  • Logistics: tracking orders, keeping inventory, managing pickup times

If any part fails, customers may not receive the value they expected.

Supply Chain Risks

Supply chains can be disrupted by supplier delays, price increases, low-quality materials, shortages, transportation problems, weather events, technology failures, or unexpected demand. Businesses need backup plans — if your first supplier runs out of packaging, you need to know where else to buy it.

Business Canvas Connection

Supply Chain Response Frame:

Our business needs __________ as key inputs. We will get these inputs from __________. We will produce or prepare our product by __________ and deliver it to customers through __________.

Example: Our business needs snacks, packaging, labels, and a payment system as key inputs. We will get these inputs from a wholesale store and an online packaging supplier. We will prepare snack boxes before after-school activities and deliver them to customers through a pickup station near the gym.

Key Takeaways

  • A supply chain is the system used to create and deliver products to customers.
  • Inputs are the resources needed to create a product or service.
  • Suppliers provide resources businesses need.
  • Production turns inputs into goods or services.
  • Distribution moves the product to the customer.
  • Logistics manages movement, timing, inventory, and delivery.
  • Strong supply chains help businesses create value reliably.

Check Your Understanding

  1. What is a supply chain?
  2. What are inputs?
  3. What does a supplier do?
  4. What is production?
  5. What is distribution?
  6. What is logistics?
  7. Give one example of a supply chain risk.
  8. How can a weak supply chain hurt customer value?
  9. Choose a business idea and list three inputs it would need.
  10. How would your business deliver its product or service to customers?

Unit 1 Review

Businesses, Competition, and New Ideas

Unit 1 Big Ideas

Unit 1 focused on how businesses begin. Strong business ideas start with customers, not products. Before a business can succeed, it must understand what problem it solves, who the customer is, how it creates and captures value, how it competes, what outside factors affect it, what values guide it, what ethical issues may arise, how work will be organized, and how the product will reach customers.

Business Canvas Checkpoint

Business Canvas Section What You Should Have
Customer Identification and validation of a customer problem, need, or want.
Product Product or service idea that achieves problem-solution fit.
Market Competitive advantage plan and PESTEL analysis.
Organization Mission statement, vision statement, and role responsibilities.
Operations Supply chain plan and production or delivery process.

Unit 1 Key Terms

Term Meaning
Business An organization that produces and distributes goods or services.
Customer The person or organization that buys a good or service.
Consumer The person who uses a good or service.
Value Creation Providing a product that responds to customer problems, needs, or wants.
Value Capture Earning revenue from the value created.
Problem-Solution Fit When a product effectively addresses a real customer problem.
Market A physical or virtual space where buyers and sellers interact.
Competitive Advantage The ability to outperform rivals in the same market.
PESTEL A framework for analyzing political, economic, social, technological, environmental, and legal factors.
Entrepreneur A person who develops a new business and accepts risk and potential reward.
Hypothesis An educated guess that can be tested.
MVP The simplest version of a product used to collect feedback.
Core Values Beliefs and principles that guide actions.
Core Competencies Skills and capabilities that help a business compete.
Mission Statement A statement explaining what a business does and how it serves customers.
Vision Statement A statement explaining what a business hopes to become.
Business Ethics Principles that guide right and wrong behavior in business.

Unit 1 Reflection

  1. What customer problem, need, or want is your business addressing?
  2. What evidence do you have that this problem is real?
  3. How does your product or service create value?
  4. How might your business capture value?
  5. Who are your main competitors or alternatives?
  6. What is your competitive advantage?
  7. Which PESTEL factor creates the biggest opportunity for your business?
  8. Which PESTEL factor creates the biggest risk?
  9. What values should guide your business?
  10. What ethical issue might your business face?
  11. What roles or responsibilities are needed to operate your business?
  12. What supply chain steps are needed to deliver your product or service?

Final Unit 1 Reminder

Do not treat your first business idea as perfect. The purpose of Unit 1 is to investigate, test, revise, and improve. Strong entrepreneurs do not simply defend their first idea — they use evidence to make it better.
