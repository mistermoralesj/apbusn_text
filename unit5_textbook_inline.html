Lesson 5.1: Taxes, Net Income, and Budgeting

Understanding Paychecks, Taxes, Spending Plans, and Financial Goals

Why This Lesson Matters

Most people spend years learning how to earn money but very little time learning how to manage it. Financial success is not determined only by how much a person earns — it is also influenced by how effectively they manage taxes, expenses, savings, and financial goals. Every financial decision involves trade-offs.

Essential Question

How can understanding taxes, net income, and budgeting help people make better financial decisions?

Gross Income vs. Net Income

Income is money received from work, investments, business ownership, government programs, or other sources. Financial decisions should be based on how much money is actually available after deductions — not on gross earnings.

Income Type Definition
Gross Income Total amount earned before taxes and deductions.
Net Income Amount remaining after taxes and deductions are removed.

Example Paycheck

Item Amount
Gross Pay $1,000
Federal Taxes −$120
Social Security −$62
Medicare −$15
Net Pay $803

The employee earned $1,000 but takes home $803. Financial planning should focus on net income rather than gross income.

Common Types of Taxes

Tax Purpose
Income Tax Funds government operations
Sales Tax Collected on purchases
Property Tax Funds local services
Payroll Taxes Supports Social Security and Medicare

Social Security helps provide income for retirees, disabled individuals, and survivors of deceased workers. Medicare helps provide healthcare coverage for many older adults and certain individuals with disabilities. Both are automatically withheld from most paychecks.

Budgeting Basics

A budget is a plan for how income will be spent, saved, and invested. It helps people control spending, reach financial goals, reduce stress, avoid excessive debt, and prepare for emergencies. Budgets include fixed expenses (rent, car payment, insurance) and variable expenses (entertainment, food, clothing).

Needs vs. Wants and Opportunity Cost

Needs are items required for basic living — housing, basic transportation, food. Wants improve quality of life but are not essential — luxury apartments, luxury vehicles, daily restaurant meals. Every financial decision also involves an opportunity cost: spending $500 on a gaming console may mean delaying a laptop purchase.

Short-Term and Long-Term Financial Goals

Budgets should support financial goals. Short-term goals are achieved within one year (new phone, vacation). Long-term goals are achieved over multiple years (college, home purchase, retirement).

Financial Advisor Project Connection

Understanding taxes, net income, and budgeting provides the foundation for helping clients make informed financial decisions throughout Unit 5.

Key Takeaways

  • Gross income is earned before deductions; net income is take-home pay.
  • Taxes fund public services and government programs.
  • Social Security and Medicare are common payroll deductions.
  • A budget is a plan for managing income and expenses.
  • Fixed expenses stay stable; variable expenses change.
  • Financial decisions involve opportunity costs.
  • Budgets should support short-term and long-term goals.

Lesson 5.2: Insurance and Risk Management

Protecting Financial Resources Through Risk Management and Insurance

Why This Lesson Matters

Unexpected events — car accidents, medical emergencies, house fires, lawsuits — can cost thousands or hundreds of thousands of dollars. Most people cannot afford these costs on their own. Insurance helps individuals transfer some of that financial risk to an insurance company.

Essential Question

How does insurance help individuals manage financial risk?

Risk Management Strategies

Risk is the possibility that an event may cause financial loss. Risk management is the process of identifying, evaluating, and responding to potential risks.

Strategy Example
Avoid Risk Choosing not to ride a motorcycle
Reduce Risk Wearing a seatbelt
Accept Risk Paying for a small repair yourself
Transfer Risk Purchasing insurance

Key Insurance Terms

Insurance is a financial product that helps protect individuals from large financial losses through risk pooling — each person pays a small amount regularly, and the insurer helps pay when a covered loss occurs.

Term Definition
Premium Amount paid for insurance coverage.
Deductible Amount paid out of pocket before insurance contributes.
Claim Request for payment after a covered loss.
Coverage Protection provided by the policy.
Policy The insurance contract.

Higher deductibles usually result in lower premiums; lower deductibles usually result in higher premiums.

Types of Insurance

Health insurance helps pay for medical expenses — doctor visits, hospital stays, emergency services, prescription medications, and preventive care. Without it, a serious illness or injury can create significant financial hardship.

Auto insurance protects against losses related to vehicle ownership. Most states require minimum liability coverage. Common types include liability, collision, comprehensive, and uninsured motorist coverage.

Homeowners insurance helps protect homes and personal property. Renters insurance protects personal belongings for people who rent. Both may cover losses from fire, theft, storm damage, and vandalism.

Life insurance provides financial support to beneficiaries after the insured person's death — replacing lost income, paying debts, and supporting dependents. It becomes more important when others depend on your income.

Disability insurance provides income if a person cannot work due to illness or injury. For many workers, their ability to earn income is their most valuable financial asset. Liability insurance helps protect against legal responsibility for damages or injuries caused to others.

Financial Advisor Project Connection

As a financial advisor, you will evaluate the risks facing your assigned client and determine what types of insurance coverage may help protect their financial future.

Key Takeaways

  • Risk is the possibility of financial loss.
  • Insurance helps transfer financial risk through risk pooling.
  • Premiums are payments made for insurance coverage.
  • Deductibles are costs paid before insurance contributes.
  • Health, auto, homeowners/renters, life, and disability insurance protect against different risks.
  • Liability insurance helps protect against legal responsibility.
  • Insurance supports long-term financial stability.

Lesson 5.3: Banking and Financial Services

Managing Money Through Banks, Credit Unions, and Financial Institutions

Why This Lesson Matters

Most people interact with financial institutions regularly — depositing paychecks, paying bills electronically, making purchases with debit cards, transferring money digitally. Understanding how banking services work helps consumers protect their money, avoid unnecessary fees, and make informed financial decisions.

Essential Question

How do financial institutions help individuals manage and protect their money?

Banks vs. Credit Unions

A bank is a for-profit financial institution that accepts deposits, provides loans, and offers financial services. Banks earn money primarily by lending deposited funds to borrowers and charging interest. A credit union is a nonprofit financial institution owned by its members, often offering lower fees, lower loan interest rates, higher savings rates, and more personalized service.

Key Account Types

Account Type Purpose Earns Interest?
Checking Account Everyday spending — debit cards, bill pay, direct deposit Little or none
Savings Account Store money for future use; emergency funds; short-term goals Yes

An emergency fund is money set aside for unexpected expenses — car repairs, medical bills, home repairs, temporary loss of income. Many financial experts recommend maintaining several months of living expenses in an emergency fund.

Electronic Banking

A debit card spends money directly from a checking account — no borrowing, no interest, but account balance decreases immediately. Direct deposit electronically transfers money into a bank account; employers commonly use it to pay employees. ACH transfers (Automated Clearing House) move money electronically between accounts — direct deposit, automatic bill payments, bank-to-bank transfers, and subscriptions.

Online and mobile banking allow customers to check balances, deposit checks, pay bills, and transfer funds. Consumers should use strong passwords, enable two-factor authentication, monitor activity, and avoid public Wi-Fi for banking. An overdraft occurs when spending exceeds available funds — banks may decline, allow with a fee, or transfer from a linked account.

Deposit Protection

FDIC (Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation) protects deposits at insured banks. NCUA (National Credit Union Administration) protects deposits at insured credit unions. These protections help maintain confidence in the financial system.

Financial Advisor Project Connection

As a financial advisor, you will evaluate your client's banking needs — recommending checking accounts, savings accounts, emergency funds, or other banking services based on their financial situation and goals.

Key Takeaways

  • Banks are for-profit institutions; credit unions are member-owned nonprofits.
  • Checking accounts support everyday spending; savings accounts help save and earn interest.
  • Debit cards spend money directly from checking accounts.
  • Direct deposit and ACH transfers move money electronically.
  • Emergency funds should cover several months of living expenses.
  • FDIC and NCUA insurance help protect deposits.
  • Consumers should compare financial institutions before opening accounts.

Lesson 5.4: Credit and Debt Management

Borrowing Money, Building Credit, Managing Debt, and Protecting Financial Health

Why This Lesson Matters

Most major purchases cannot be paid for entirely with cash. Credit can be a powerful financial tool when used responsibly. However, excessive debt, missed payments, and poor credit decisions can create long-term financial problems.

Essential Question

How can individuals use credit responsibly while avoiding harmful debt?

Credit, Debt, and the Good/Bad Distinction

Credit is the ability to borrow money or access goods and services with the promise of paying later. Debt is money owed. Debt itself is not automatically good or bad — it depends on how borrowed money is used and whether the borrower can repay responsibly.

Potentially Helpful Debt Risky Debt
Education loans — builds future income potential High-interest consumer debt
Mortgage — builds equity in an asset Unnecessary luxury purchases on credit
Business investment loan Repeated credit card balances for entertainment

Even "helpful" debt can become problematic if it exceeds a person's ability to repay.

Credit Reports and Credit Scores

A credit report records a person's borrowing and repayment history — open accounts, loan balances, payment history, credit inquiries, and collection accounts. A credit score is a numerical estimate of creditworthiness that helps lenders predict repayment likelihood.

Score Range General Rating
800+ Excellent
740–799 Very Good
670–739 Good
580–669 Fair
Below 580 Poor

The five main factors: Payment History (most important), Credit Utilization, Length of Credit History, Types of Credit, and New Credit Applications.

Common Credit Products

Credit cards allow borrowing up to a limit and offer convenience, fraud protection, and rewards — but balances carried month-to-month accrue interest. APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the yearly cost of borrowing. A 29% APR card costs significantly more than a 15% APR card when balances aren't paid in full. Paying only the minimum payment slows debt reduction and maximizes interest paid.

Student loans help pay educational expenses — borrowers should consider total amount, expected income after graduation, repayment terms, and interest rates. Auto loans finance vehicle purchases; longer terms reduce monthly payments but increase total interest. A mortgage is a loan for real estate — often the largest financial obligation a household will carry.

The debt-to-income ratio (DTI) compares monthly debt payments to monthly income. Lower DTI generally indicates stronger financial health and improves chances of loan approval. Identity theft occurs when someone uses another person's information without permission — consumers should monitor accounts and credit reports regularly.

Financial Advisor Project Connection

As a financial advisor, you will evaluate a client's debt obligations, credit situation, borrowing decisions, and repayment strategies. Your recommendations should help clients improve long-term financial stability while minimizing unnecessary borrowing costs.

Key Takeaways

  • Credit allows individuals to borrow money and pay later.
  • Credit reports and credit scores influence borrowing opportunities.
  • Payment history is one of the most important credit score factors.
  • APR represents the yearly cost of borrowing.
  • Credit cards, student loans, auto loans, and mortgages are common credit types.
  • Debt-to-income ratio helps lenders evaluate borrowing risk.
  • Responsible borrowing supports long-term financial success.

Lesson 5.5: Saving and Investing

Building Wealth Through Compound Growth, Diversification, and Long-Term Planning

Why This Lesson Matters

Earning money is only one part of financial success. Individuals must also decide what to do with the money they earn. Understanding the difference between saving and investing helps individuals prepare for both the short and long term.

Essential Question

How can saving and investing help individuals achieve long-term financial goals?

Saving vs. Investing

Saving Investing
Focuses on safety and accessibility. Focuses on long-term growth.
Lower risk; lower potential return. Higher risk; higher potential return.
Often used for emergencies and short-term goals. Often used for wealth building and retirement.

Simple vs. Compound Interest

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal. At 5% annual interest, $1,000 earns $50 per year — always based on the original $1,000. Compound interest earns interest on both the original principal and interest already accumulated. This accelerates growth over time — Albert Einstein is often credited with calling compound interest one of the most powerful forces in finance.

Inflation is the general increase in prices over time. As inflation rises, purchasing power decreases. One reason people invest is to grow wealth faster than inflation. The time value of money is the idea that money available today is generally worth more than the same amount in the future because it can be invested and grow.

Example: The Power of Starting Early

Student A invests $2,000 at age 18. Student B waits until age 28 to invest the same amount. Even earning the same rate of return, Student A will likely accumulate significantly more wealth because the money has more time to compound.

Common Investment Types

A stock represents ownership in a company — stockholders may benefit from price increases and dividend payments. Stocks offer strong long-term growth potential but can experience significant short-term fluctuations. A bond is a loan made by an investor to a government or organization — investors receive regular interest payments and principal repayment at maturity. Bonds are generally less volatile than stocks but offer lower long-term returns.

A mutual fund pools money from many investors and invests in a collection of assets, with professional management providing diversification and convenience. ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds) trade on stock exchanges like stocks but hold multiple investments — popular for diversification and lower fees.

Diversification and Risk vs. Return

Diversification means spreading investments across multiple assets to reduce risk. The idea: don't put all your eggs in one basket. A diversified portfolio may include stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs, and cash savings.

Investment Type General Risk Level Return Potential
Savings Account Low Low
Bonds Low to Moderate Moderate
Stocks Moderate to High Higher

Financial Advisor Project Connection

As a financial advisor, you will evaluate a client's goals, risk tolerance, and time horizon. Your recommendations should explain how saving and investing strategies can help the client achieve long-term financial objectives.

Key Takeaways

  • Saving focuses on safety and accessibility; investing focuses on growth.
  • Compound interest allows money to grow faster over time.
  • The earlier investments begin, the more time they have to compound.
  • Inflation reduces purchasing power over time.
  • Stocks represent ownership; bonds represent loans to organizations.
  • Mutual funds and ETFs provide diversification.
  • Investment decisions should align with financial goals and risk tolerance.

Lesson 5.6: Retirement Planning and Building Long-Term Wealth

Financial Independence, Retirement Accounts, Long-Term Investing, and Wealth Building Strategies

Why This Lesson Matters

Most people spend decades earning income from work. Eventually they may want or need to stop working. Individuals who begin planning early often have greater flexibility, more financial security, and fewer financial challenges later in life. Building wealth is usually the result of consistent habits repeated over many years — not a single financial decision.

Essential Question

How can long-term financial planning help individuals achieve financial independence and retirement security?

Retirement and Financial Independence

Retirement is the stage of life when an individual stops working full-time and relies on accumulated savings, investments, pensions, or other income sources. Retirement can last twenty to thirty years or more. Financial independence occurs when a person's assets and income sources generate enough to support their lifestyle without relying primarily on employment.

Sources of Retirement Income

Source Description
Personal Savings Money set aside over time
Investments Stocks, bonds, ETFs, mutual funds
Employer Retirement Plans 401(k), 403(b), and similar plans
Individual Retirement Accounts Traditional IRA and Roth IRA
Social Security Government retirement benefits

Employer-Sponsored Plans and Matching

Many employers offer 401(k) plans where employees contribute a portion of their paycheck into investment accounts with favorable tax treatment. Some employers match contributions — if an employee contributes 5% and the employer matches 5%, that's a 100% return on that contribution before any market gains. Many financial advisors consider employer matching one of the most valuable workplace benefits available.

An Individual Retirement Account (IRA) allows individuals to save and invest for retirement independently of an employer. Common types: Traditional IRA (tax-deductible contributions, taxes paid on withdrawal) and Roth IRA (after-tax contributions, tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement).

Net Worth and Building Wealth

Net worth measures overall financial position: Assets − Liabilities = Net Worth. Assets include cash, savings, investments, real estate, and business ownership. Liabilities include credit card balances, student loans, auto loans, and mortgages. Increasing assets and reducing liabilities grows wealth.

Lifestyle inflation occurs when spending increases as income increases — buying increasingly expensive vehicles, larger homes than necessary, or spending raises instead of saving them. While enjoying higher income is reasonable, excessive lifestyle inflation slows wealth building.

Financial Habits That Build Wealth

  • Living below one's means.
  • Maintaining an emergency fund.
  • Investing consistently.
  • Avoiding high-interest debt.
  • Taking advantage of employer retirement plans.
  • Diversifying investments.
  • Monitoring financial goals regularly.

Business Canvas Connection

Financial Advisor Project Connection:

Your final financial plan should explain how your client can build long-term wealth while balancing risk, savings, debt management, and retirement preparation. Strong recommendations should be realistic, evidence-based, and aligned with the client's goals.

Key Takeaways

  • Retirement planning prepares individuals for life after full-time employment.
  • Financial independence occurs when assets generate sufficient income to support lifestyle needs.
  • Social Security, investments, retirement plans, and savings provide retirement income.
  • Employer matching contributions can significantly increase retirement savings.
  • Net worth equals assets minus liabilities.
  • Starting early allows compound growth to work over longer periods.
  • Building wealth requires consistent financial habits.

Financial Advisor Project

Capstone Project for Unit 5

Project Overview

In this project, you will assume the role of a professional financial advisor. You will analyze a client profile and develop a personalized financial plan designed to improve the client's financial well-being and help them achieve their goals. Recommendations should be realistic, evidence-based, and aligned with the information provided in the case.

Required Areas of Analysis

Financial Area Recommendation Required
Budgeting Evaluate spending and savings
Insurance Evaluate risk protection needs
Banking Recommend appropriate financial services
Debt Evaluate borrowing and repayment strategies
Investing Recommend investment approaches
Retirement Evaluate long-term retirement readiness

Characteristics of Strong Recommendations

Strong recommendations are based on evidence from the client profile, specific and actionable, supported by financial concepts learned in class, realistic and achievable, and connected to the client's goals.

Presentation Expectations

Your final presentation should explain: (1) The client's current financial situation. (2) The client's strengths and challenges. (3) Your recommendations. (4) The reasoning behind each recommendation. (5) The expected impact of the plan.
